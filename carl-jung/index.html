Carl "C.G." Jung

founder of analytical psychology

Carl "C.G." was one of the most brilliant minds in psychology

Important dates in the life of Jung:

  • 1875 - Born in Switzerland
  • 1895 - Began studying medicine at the University of Basel
  • 1896 - Jung’s father, Paul, died
  • 1990 - Began working in a psychiatric hospital in Zurich
  • 1903 - Married Emma Rauschenbach
  • 1906 - Jung met Sigmund Freud for the first time, initiating a close relationship
  • 1912 - Jung published “Psychology of the Unconscious”. Freud rejected Jung’s ideas which eventually led to the dissolution of their friendship
  • 1915 - Drafted as an army doctor in World War I
  • 1921 - Published one of his best known works “Psychological Types”
  • 1932 - Awarded Zurich’s literature prize
  • 1955 - His wife, Emma, died
  • 1959 - Jung published his last book “Flying Saucers: A Modern Myth of Things Seen in the Skies”
  • 1961 - Jung died
  • 1963 - “Memories, Dreams, Reflections”, a partially autobiographical work, is published reflecting on Jung’s life
  • 2009 - The Red Book was published which was a personal journal of Jung’s “confrontation with the unconscious” (hallucinations) which he kept record of from 1913 to 1929.

Important psychological concepts introduced by Jung:

  • Synchronicity
    • events are "meaningful coincidences" if they occur with no causal relationship yet seem to be meaningfully related
  • Archetypal phenomena
    • universal, mythic characters that reside within the collective unconscious of people the world over
  • Collective unconcious
    • the part of the unconscious mind that is derived from ancestral memory and experience and is common to all humankind, as distinct from the individual's unconscious
  • Psychological complex
    • a core pattern of emotions, memories, perceptions, and wishes in the personal unconscious organized around a common theme, such as power or status
  • Introversion and Extraversion
    • refers to where people get their energy and motivation from: other people or within themselves
